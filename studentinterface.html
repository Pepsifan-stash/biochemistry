Lipid Metabolism: The Body's Energy Reservoir

Unit 1: Digestion, Transport, and Beta-Oxidation

Phase 1: Digestion

Breaking the Drop

Lipids (fats) are hydrophobic, meaning they don't mix with water. To be metabolized, they must first be Emulsified by bile salts in the small intestine. This turns large fat globules into tiny droplets, increasing the surface area for enzymes called lipases to work.

The Goal: Break complex Triacylglycerols (TAGs) down into free fatty acids and glycerol so they can pass through the intestinal wall.
Knowledge Check: Why can't the body just absorb a large fat globule directly into the bloodstream like it does with dissolved glucose?
Phase 2: Transport

The Chylomicron Shuttle

Once inside the intestinal cells, fats are rebuilt and packaged into "suitcases" called Chylomicrons. These are a type of lipoprotein that allows hydrophobic fats to travel through the watery environment of the lymph system and eventually the blood.

Did you know? Unlike carbs and proteins, fats bypass the liver initially and enter the "drainage system" (lymphatic system) first.
Critical Thinking: If a patient has a "fatty liver," does that suggest a problem with fat storage or fat transport?
Phase 3: Catabolism

Beta-Oxidation: The "Slicing" Process

To get energy, the body moves fatty acids into the mitochondria using the Carnitine Shuttle. Once inside, the process of Beta-Oxidation begins. This pathway "slices" two carbons off the long fatty acid chain at a time.

The Yield: Each 2-carbon slice becomes Acetyl-CoA, which enters the Krebs Cycle to produce massive amounts of ATP (energy).
Math Connection: Glucose has 6 carbons. A typical fatty acid might have 16 carbons. Which molecule do you think provides more "bang for your buck" in terms of ATP production?
