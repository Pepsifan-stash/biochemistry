Biological Lipids

An Advanced Introduction based on Lehninger Principles of Biochemistry (8th Ed)

1. Storage Lipids: Triacylglycerols

Storage lipids are derivatives of fatty acids—hydrocarbon derivatives with carboxylic acid head groups. In Lehninger, we focus on Triacylglycerols (fats). They consist of three fatty acids in ester linkage with a single glycerol.

  • Function: Energy storage and insulation.
  • Chemistry: They are nonpolar, hydrophobic, and chemically inert.

2. Membrane Structural Lipids

Unlike storage lipids, membrane lipids are amphipathic: one end is hydrophobic (tails), and the other is hydrophilic (polar head). Lehninger classifies these into three main types:

  • Glycerophospholipids: Hydrophobic regions composed of two fatty acids joined to glycerol.
  • Galactolipids: Predominant in plant cells (chloroplasts).
  • Sphingolipids: No glycerol; they use sphingosine, a long-chain amino alcohol.

3. Signaling and Pigment Lipids

While some lipids store energy or build walls, others act as signals. These are present in much smaller amounts.

  • Sterols: Structural lipids present in the membranes of most eukaryotic cells (e.g., Cholesterol).
  • Eicosanoids: Paracrine hormones that act only on cells near their point of synthesis.
  • Vitamins: Lipids such as A, D, E, and K are essential for biological function but cannot be synthesized by vertebrates.
